Bayesian Inference of Mutational Cell Fractions¶

Notations¶

  • $\phi$ mutation cell fraction
  • $m$ mutation multiplicity
  • $b$ number of variant reads
  • $d$ sequencing depth or total number of reads
  • $c_t$ overall total copy number of the tumor at the mutation locus
  • $c_n$ total copy number of the normal at the mutation locus ($c_n = 1$ if mutation on X else 2)
  • $\rho$ tumor purity
  • $\xi$ variant allele fraction (VAF). Note: This is NOT the observed VAF from sampling reads from the locus, but the true underlying fraction of mutant alleles out of all alleles present in the gene pool

Any bayesian inference has three components: prior, likehood, and a posterior. They are three key components of the Bayes Rule.

$$ p(A|B) = \frac{p(B|A)p(A)}{p(B)}$$

Where $p(A|B)$ is the posterior, $p(B|A)$ the likelihood, and $p(A)$ the prior. $p(B)$ is the normalization factorm, but we often disregard it when our primary goal is optimization. Hence

$$ posterior \propto likelihood \cdot prior$$

In the problem of mutational cell fraction, we first make an inference of the underlying variant allele fraction, then indirectly infer the mutational cell fraction. Bayesian inference allows us to quanfity the uncertainty of the value estimates.

Likelihood¶

When we draw reads from the gene pool, we try to get a sense of what the underlying variant allele fraction is. The likelihood of observing $b$ variant reads in $d$ total reads is a binomial distribution where the probability of success is $\xi$:

$$p(b | d, \xi) = Binom(b; d, \xi(\phi))$$

Piror¶

Let's say that we don't have any prior belief for $\xi$ to be any specific value from 0 to 1, so we choose a uniform (beta) prior for $\xi$ with $\alpha = 1$ and $\beta = 1$: $$p(\xi) = Beta(\xi; 1,1)$$

Posterior¶

This gives a posterior belief about $\xi$, which follows a Beta distribution:

\begin{align*} p(\xi | b, d) &= \frac{p(b | d, \xi) p(\xi)}{p(b | d)} \\ &= \frac{Binom(b; d, \xi) Beta(\xi; 1,1)}{\int_0^1 Binom(b; d, \xi) d\xi} \\ &= Beta(\xi; b + 1, d - b +1) \\ \end{align*}

This is because the beta distribution is the conjugate prior of the binomial distribution. Let's see how different observed VAFs gives different posteriors for $\xi$:

Notice that maximum likelihood of $\xi$ is exactly $\frac{b}{d}$.

$$E(\xi | b, d) = \frac{b}{d}$$

Transformation¶

Then, we want to infer the value of $\phi$ from the posterior distribution of $\xi$ that we got from the sequencing experiment. We can view $\xi$ as a linear function of $\phi$:

$$\xi \equiv \phi \cdot \frac{\rho \ m}{(1-\rho) \ c_n + \rho \ c_t}$$

Assuming that the other parameters ($\rho, c_n, c_t, m$) are given, $\frac{\rho \ m}{(1-\rho) \ c_n + \rho \ c_t}$ is a constant. Let's denote this constant scaling factor as $C \equiv \frac{\rho \ m}{(1 - \rho) \ c_n + \rho \ c_t}$. Then

$$\xi = C \cdot \phi$$

Then there is a linear map $g$ from $\xi$ to $\phi$:

$$\phi = g(\xi) = \xi \cdot \frac{1}{C}$$

And since we know the posterior distribution of $\xi$, we apply the Change of Variables Rule to obtain the posterior distribution of $\phi$.

\begin{align*} p(\phi) &= \Bigg|\frac{d}{d\phi} \big( g^{-1} (\phi) \big) \Bigg| \cdot Beta(g^{-1}(\phi); b + 1, d - b + 1) \\ &= \Bigg|\frac{d}{d\phi} \big( C \cdot \phi \big) \Bigg| \cdot Beta(C \cdot \phi; b + 1, d - b + 1) \\ &= C \cdot Beta(C \cdot \phi; b + 1, d - b + 1) \end{align*}

def C(m, rho, c_t, c_n):
    return (rho * m) / ((1-rho)*c_n + rho * c_t)

def posterior_phi(phi, b, d, m, rho, c_t, c_n):
    return C(m, rho, c_t, c_n) * beta.pdf(C(m, rho, c_t, c_n) * phi, b + 1, d - b + 1)

def print_params(params):
    return str(params).strip("{|}").replace("'", "").replace(':', ' =')

A Note about Point Estimate of $\phi$¶

If we just want a single point estimate for $\phi$, namely the conditional expectation of $\phi$ given the observed variables

$$\hat{\phi} = E(\phi | m, b, d, c_t, c_n, \rho) = \int \phi \ p(\phi | m, b, d, c_t, c_n, \rho) \ d\phi$$

we can apply the Law of the Unconscious Statistician

\begin{align} E(\phi | m, b, d, c_t, c_n, \rho) &= \int \phi \ p(\phi | m, b, d, c_t, c_n, \rho) \ d\phi \\ &= \int g(\xi) p(\xi | m, b, d, c_t, c_n, \rho) \ d \xi \\ &= \int g(\xi) p(\xi | b, d) \ d \xi \\ &= \int \frac{1}{C} \ \xi \ p(\xi | b, d) \ d \xi \\ &= \frac{1}{C} \int \xi \ p(\xi | b, d) \ d \xi \\ &= \frac{1}{C} E(\xi| b, d) \\ &= \frac{1}{C} \frac{b}{d} \end{align}

Expand $C$ and we get

$$ \hat{\phi} = \frac{1}{m} \cdot \frac{b}{d} \cdot \frac{(1-\rho) \ c_n + \rho \ c_t}{\rho} $$

Which is exactly Eq. 4 in Principles of reconstructing the subclonal archeitecture of cancers!
